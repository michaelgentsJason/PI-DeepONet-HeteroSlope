               To address these limitations, hybrid approaches embedding physical principles into ML models have gained
               traction (Karniadakis et al., 2021; Karpatne et al., 2017). Among these, physics–informed neural networks
               (PINNs) incorporate governing equations directly into the loss function, ensuring consistency between model
               outputs, observed data, and underlying physics (Raissi et al., 2019). Supported by automatic differentiation (AD)
               in modern DL frameworks, PINNs have found success in solving partial differential equations, notably in fluid
               mechanics and beyond (Abadi et al., 2016; Cai et al., 2021; Cuomo et al., 2022; Paszke et al., 2019). These
               advantages have naturally attracted attention in subsurface flow modeling. For example, DA can leverage
               physics–informed ML as an alternative optimization method (Yeung et al., 2022) or employ it as a surrogate
               model for forward simulations (N. Wang et al., 2021b). Meanwhile, PINNs can be applied directly without
               explicit forward solvers, making them particularly suitable for problems with uncertain or only partially known
               conditions.
               Motivated by these features, researchers have begun exploring PINNs for unsaturated flow. Tartakovsky
               et al. (2020) pioneered PINNs to capture the hydraulic conductivity function (HCF) in homogeneous, steady–state
               unsaturated flow. Bandai and Ghezzehei (2021) introduced monotonic neural networks to represent both the
               water retention curve (WRC) and HCF, preserving their inherent monotonicity. Depina et al. (2022) extended
               PINNs to inverse problems by integrating the van Genuchten–Mualem constitutive model (Mualem, 1976; Van
               Genuchten, 1980). Further advancements addressed specific challenges in unsaturated flow modeling. Bandai and
               Ghezzehei (2022) developed a domain decomposition strategy for PINNs to invert surface flux. Y. Wang, Wang,
               et al. (2023) enhanced soil moisture prediction by incorporating both the RRE and water balance constraints.
               Bandai et al. (2024) further showed that PINNs could invert constitutive relationships with greater flexibility than
               traditional parameterized models. Recent studies have expanded PINNs to more complex and higher dimensional
               scenarios, including incorporating hydrothermal processes to improve water flux estimates (Y. Wang, Shi,
               et al., 2023), integrating geoelectric data for solute transport modeling (Haruzi & Moreno, 2023), and charac-
               terizing two–dimensional soil water dynamics in heterogeneous soils (Sakar et al., 2024).
               Despite these notable efforts, to the best of our knowledge, two critical aspects of real–world unsaturated systems
               have received limited attention when considered jointly in PINN–based modeling. First, uncertainty in the uBC is
               frequently simplified, with many studies assuming well–defined or perfectly known uBC (sometimes even a
               simple Dirichlet or time–independent boundary) (Depina et al., 2022; Y. Chen et al., 2023; Oikawa & Saito, 2024;
               Sakar et al., 2024). Second, although some investigations account for soil heterogeneity, they often rely on
               simplified hydraulic architectures, such as layered or effectively homogeneous representations (Bandai &
               Ghezzehei, 2022; Elkhadrawi et al., 2024; Oikawa & Saito, 2024). The combined effect of uncertain uBC and
               strong spatial variability in soil properties thus remains underexplored. Addressing this gap is crucial for real–
               world applications, where atmospheric forcing is rarely perfectly known and soil hydraulic parameters can
               vary considerably at multiple scales.
               In contrast, significant progress has been made in groundwater (saturated) flow, where geostatistical approaches
               have been integrated with PINNs to handle spatial heterogeneity (J. Li & Tartakovsky, 2022; N. Wang
               et al., 2020; N. Wang et al., 2021a, 2021b; N. Wang et al., 2024; Q. Zheng et al., 2020). Particularly, Ji
               et al. (2024) combined the Karhunen–Loève Expansion (KLE) with PINN (KLE–PINN) to perform hydraulic
               tomography under missing boundary cases. KLE is likewise widely used to represent soil heterogeneity (Das
               et al., 2010; Shibata et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2004), suggesting its potential applicability to unsaturated flow.
               However, while KLE–PINN has proven effective in groundwater contexts, unsaturated flow poses additional
               challenges due to higher nonlinearity and uncertain, dynamic atmospheric forcing. Whether this framework can
               replicate its success in unsaturated flow—where the RRE constraint is more nonlinear and uBC is far more
               variable—remains an open question.

               Furthermore, while PINNs have demonstrated the potential to handle uncertain boundary conditions, this
               capability is not exclusive to them. Traditional DA methods have long been developed, at least in principle, to
               address uncertainties in both model parameters and boundary conditions (Gillijns & Moor, 2007). Thus, a natural

               2.2. PINNs
               2.2.1. Motivation and Comparison

               Multi–layer perceptrons (MLPs) are powerful tools for function approximation (Cybenko, 1989; Hornik
               et al., 1989). In PINNs, the central concept is to combine a data–driven loss term with a physics–based loss term,
               the latter evaluated via AD in DL frameworks such as PyTorch (Ketkar & Moolayil, 2021; Paszke et al., 2019).
               However, training PINNs can be challenging when dealing with complex partial differential equations, because of
               gradient flow pathologies that can impede convergence (S. Wang et al., 2021). To mitigate this, we adopt a
               modified MLP architecture (see Appendix A).
               Building on the Karhunen–Loève Expansion (KLE, detailed in Appendix B), we integrate KLE with PINN to
               form the KLE–PINN architecture (Figure 1a). In essence, KLE–PINN has two main components: one network
               maps spatiotemporal coordinates to state variables (e.g., matric potential ψ), while another encodes the hetero-
               geneous parameter field via a finite set of random coefficients. This setup has demonstrated promise in addressing
               parameter heterogeneity in groundwater flow (Ji et al., 2024; J. Li & Tartakovsky, 2022; N. Wang et al., 2021a; N.
               Wang et al., 2024).
               However, to the best of our knowledge, in these existing KLE–PINN frameworks for direct inversion, parameter
               heterogeneity typically influences the states only through the physics–based loss term. Such an indirect coupling
               may not fully leverage the rich information embedded in scarce observations, as the parameter–state relationships
               are mediated solely through the PDE–based loss term rather than being directly learned from data.
               To address this limitation, we propose three different architectures:
               1. Direct Parameter–State Coupling (KLE–PINN–C) A straightforward remedy is to directly feed the KLE–
                  derived parameter features into the PINN as additional inputs, thereby explicitly coupling parameters and
                  states. We refer to this as KLE–PINN with direct state–parameter coupling (KLE–PINN–C, Figure 1a). This
                  direct approach follows the intuition that explicit parameter information should help the network better capture
                  parameter–state relationships, especially when observations are limited. Although this approach seems intu-
                  itive, simply appending extra inputs can potentially introduce additional optimization challenges.
               2. Enhanced Parameter–State Coupling via Branch–Trunk (KLE–PINN–EC) While direct coupling (KLE–
                  PINN–C) provides explicit parameter information to the network, a standard MLP may still learn to bypass
                  parameter inputs and predominantly rely on spatiotemporal coordinates to predict states, especially when the
                  parameter–state relationships are complex. Drawing inspiration from DeepONet (Lu et al., 2021), we separate
                  the processing of heterogeneous parameter features (branch network) from spatiotemporal coordinates (trunk

                                        3.1.2. Implementation Details
                                        A key advantage of PINNs is their ability to implement physical constraints through collocation points—spatial
                                        and temporal locations where the governing PDE needs to be satisfied without requiring any labeled data. Unlike
                                        observation points that provide direct measurements, collocation points can be placed anywhere within the
                                        computational domain and resampled dynamically. To ensure that the RRE is satisfied throughout the entire
                                        spatiotemporal domain, a sufficiently large number of collocation points is typically required. However, main-
                                        taining a fixed large set of collocation points throughout training can be computationally expensive. A widely
                                        adopted strategy to address these challenges is dynamic resampling (Wu et al., 2023). Following this approach,
                                        we uniformly resample 4000 collocation points at each training iteration.
                                        For the inverse problem setup, we aim to estimate the unknown ln K s field of the heterogeneous soils from θ
                                        observations. Observation points were uniformly distributed in the spatial domain, with a spacing of 18 cm,
                                        ranging from z = − 5 cm to z = − 95 cm, totaling 6 spatial points. In the temporal domain, observations were
                                        recorded at intervals of 0.1 days from t = 0 to t = 10 days, resulting in 101 time points. Thus, there were 606
                                        observation points in the spatiotemporal domain.

                                        To simulate realistic conditions, noise sampled from a normal distribution was added to the observed data to form
                                        the PINN training data set. The noisy observational data is defined as:

                                            4.1.2. Effect of Incorrect Prior
                                            In practice, geostatistical priors such as variance and correlation length are often estimated from limited data, thus
                                            introducing uncertainty. To evaluate the impact of inaccuracies in these priors, we introduced deliberate errors in
                                            their values and observed how PINNs responded during inversion.
                                            Figure 4 compares the results for KLE–PINN, KLE–PINN–C, KLE–PINN–BT, and KLE–PINN–EC under
                                            incorrect prior assumptions. Although prior deviations lead to some performance fluctuations, the overall impact
                                            is moderate. Remarkably, even in the worst cases, all models except KLE–PINN–C achieve R2 values near or
                                            above 0.90, demonstrating practical robustness for field applications where precise geostatistical parameters are
                                            rarely available.
                                            Consistent with N. Wang et al. (2021a) in groundwater studies, errors in correlation length exert a more pro-
                                            nounced effect than those in variance. This occurs because correlation length determines the effective dimen-
                                            sionality (i.e., how many KLE modes are required to capture >95% of the parameter variability), whereas
                                            variance primarily rescales the eigenfunctions and eigenvalues without altering the number of KLE terms needed.
                                            Consequently, a mismatch in correlation length can impede the reconstruction of the parameter field, resulting in
                                            larger drops in accuracy.

                                            In summary, these tests provide strong evidence that the KLE–PINN framework can successfully address the dual
                                            challenges of soil heterogeneity and uncertain boundary in unsaturated flow modeling. However, the poor per-
                                            formance of the direct coupling approach (KLE–PINN–C) highlights that successful learning of parameter–state
                                            relationships requires careful consideration, rather than simply adding additional inputs.

               4.4. Limitations and Future Perspectives
               This study presents a systematic investigation of integrating PINNs and KLE to simultaneously handle uncertain
               uBC and soil parameter heterogeneity in unsaturated flow. While our results demonstrate the effectiveness of this
               approach, several aspects warrant consideration for future advancement.

               First, our analysis focused on 1D vertical flow and modeled heterogeneity solely through the saturated hydraulic
               conductivity. These choices, while enabling rigorous methodological evaluation, naturally suggest opportunities
               for extending the KLE–PINN–EC framework to higher–dimensional (2D or 3D) domains and incorporating
               additional soil properties (such as porosity). Such extensions would enable a more comprehensive representation
               of spatial heterogeneity and complex boundary interactions encountered in field–scale applications.
               Second, while our results indicate that the proposed KLE–PINN–EC method can achieve improved accuracy
               under relatively sparse observational data, scenarios with extremely limited measurements remain an important
               practical challenge. In such cases, strategies for explicitly incorporating uncertain BCs as additional soft con-
               straints could provide supplementary information to mitigate data scarcity. Investigating the optimal balance
               between BC uncertainty quantification and minimal in situ observations represents an important direction for
               enhancing the framework's practical applicability.
               Third, this study employed the widely used VGM model (Mualem, 1976; Van Genuchten, 1980) with spatially
               varying parameters to represent soil heterogeneity. Although the VGM model is well established, incorporating
               more physically–based frameworks that couple the WRC and hydraulic conductivity function via concepts like
               the Leverett J–function (Leverett, 1941) could enhance physical consistency. Rather than replacing the VGM
               model, the J–function can serve as a scaling concept that preserves the underlying form of the chosen WRC while
               adjusting its shape according to the local permeability and other factors (e.g., porosity). Future research could
               explore such coupling to achieve a more physically consistent representation of hydraulic behavior.

               These considerations highlight that the proposed KLE–PINN–EC framework provides a solid foundation that can
               be further refined and extended, enhancing its applicability to complex real–world unsaturated flow problems.
               The integration of higher–dimensional modeling, the development of strategies for severe data scarcity, and
               implementation of more comprehensive constitutive relationships all represent valuable avenues for advancing
               this methodology.

               5. Conclusions
               This study presents the integration of KLE and PINNs to address the dual challenges of soil parameter hetero-
               geneity and BC uncertainty in unsaturated flow modeling. We systematically investigated three critical questions
               that have remained unresolved:
               1. Can the integration of KLE and PINN effectively handle the combined effect of soil parameter heterogeneity
                  and uncertain uBC under the nonlinear constraints of the RRE?
               2. Does the novel KLE–PINN–EC architecture with explicit parameter–state coupling offer tangible advantages
                  over conventional KLE–PINN for reconstructing heterogeneous soil properties from sparse, noisy
                  observations?
               3. How does KLE–PINN–EC compare to a traditional DA method, ES–MDA, particularly when uBCs are
                  uncertain?

               Our key findings include:
               1. Successful joint representation of heterogeneity and uncertain uBCs. Our results demonstrate that the KLE–
                  PINN framework successfully handles both challenges simultaneously, even when the geostatistical priors
                  contain moderate biases. However, the framework is more sensitive to errors in correlation length than
                  variance, emphasizing the need for reasonably accurate spatial continuity characterization in field
                  applications.

                  and joint parameter–BC inversion is feasible, ES–MDA performs competitively with computational effi-

               Our findings suggest that traditional DA methods and physics–informed DL approaches offer complementary
               tools for subsurface characterization. ES–MDA remains highly effective when prior information is reliable, while
               the KLE–PINN–EC framework provides a more flexible and robust alternative for real–world applications where
               BCs are poorly constrained and soil heterogeneity is pronounced. Future research should extend this approach to
               more complex scenarios, such as multidimensional flow and the simultaneous modeling of multiple heteroge-
               neous parameters. As climate variability increases uncertainty in atmospheric forcing and data availability re-
               mains limited, hybrid approaches like KLE–PINN–EC will likely play an increasingly important role in
               subsurface hydrology.


               Appendix A: Structure of the Modified MLP
               To address the gradient flow pathologies commonly observed in PINNs, Wang et al. (2021) proposed a modified

               Appendix B: Karhunen–Loève Expansion
               For subsurface flow problems in heterogeneous media, the heterogeneity and uncertainty of hydraulic parameters
               can be effectively captured by modeling them as a random field (Mantoglou & Gelhar, 1987; Schad &
               Teutsch, 1994). A common and efficient way to achieve this is the Karhunen–Loève expansion (KLE), which uses
               a finite set of random coefficients to approximate the dominant modes of variability (Yang et al., 2004; D. Zhang
               & Lu, 2004). By doing so, KLE not only parameterizes the heterogeneous field but also reduces its dimen-
               sionality, making high–dimensional inverse or forward problems more tractable.

               Consider the saturated hydraulic conductivity as a realization of a Gaussian random parameter field:

               Appendix E: Sensitivity of ES–MDA to Ensemble Sizes and Assimilation Steps
               In Section 4.3.2, we observed that ES–MDA struggled to invert soil hydraulic parameters ln K s when the pre-
               scribed uBC flux was biased in both magnitude and timing (scenarios S5–S8). To ensure that this performance
               limitation is not merely due to suboptimal choices of ensemble size or assimilation steps, this appendix in-
               vestigates the sensitivity of ES–MDA to these hyperparameters.
               We varied the ensemble size over 50, 100 (baseline), 150 and 200 members. The approximate computational costs
               for these configurations were 14, 28, 42 and 56 min, respectively. Table E1 reports the final estimation accuracy
               for ln K s ; Figure E1 compares the reconstructed uBC fluxes, and Figure E2 tracks the RMSE of ln K s estimates
               throughout the DA process for each ensemble size. The results indicate that while minor variations in performance
               are observable with different ensemble sizes, these differences are not substantial enough to alter the fundamental
               finding: ES–MDA exhibits limited capability in providing accurate parameter estimations (Table E1) or recon-
               structing the uBC flux (Figure E1) in scenarios S5–S8, regardless of the ensemble size tested.
               Figure E2 further illustrates this pattern by showing consistent RMSE evolution trends across all DA steps for the
               different ensemble sizes within each scenario. For scenarios S5 and S6, the RMSE diverges after approximately
               10 steps regardless of ensemble size, indicating challenges in the assimilation process. Scenarios S7 and S8 show
               initial RMSE increases followed by partial recovery, yet final errors remain substantially higher than those
               achieved by KLE–PINN–EC. These findings suggest that simply increasing the ensemble size or adjusting the
               number of DA steps does not address the fundamental challenges encountered in these complex scenarios where
               both timing and magnitude of BCs are uncertain. The performance limitations appear to stem from the inherent
               difficulty of simultaneously resolving multiple sources of uncertainty within the ES–MDA framework, rather
               than from insufficient ensemble representation.
